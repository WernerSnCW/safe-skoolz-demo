What zero tolerance means in practice


Zero tolerance does not mean every incident leads to expulsion.
It means no incident is ignored, minimised or left without a response. Every incident must be recorded, assessed, and met with a clear and proportionate consequence. The question is not whether the school responds — it is how.


Four levels of behaviour — four levels of response

Level
Examples
Minimum response
1 — Low level
One-off unkind comment, minor argument
Conversation, logged, monitored
2 — Repeated targeting
Repeated name-calling, exclusion, and property damage
Formal log, parents notified within 48 hrs, warning, monitoring plan
3 — Serious misconduct
Physical assault, sustained bullying, and group targeting
Protocol opened, coordinator, separate interviews, 3-day suspension, behaviour contract
4 — Sexual harassment
Sexualised comments, unwanted touching, and being surrounded by sexual intent
Immediate separation, protocol, 5-day suspension, external assessment, behaviour contract. Referral to Fiscalía if criminal threshold met.


Six rules the school must always follow

1. Separate first  In any serious incident, children must be separated before anything else happens.
2. No return until safe  A child who has been harmed must not be returned to shared spaces with the perpetrator until a written risk assessment has been completed.
3. Assess the pattern  Every incident must be looked at alongside previous incidents involving the same children. Three or more incidents in a term automatically trigger a formal review.
4. Use precise language  The school must use accurate terms — including sexual harassment, sustained targeted bullying, and coercive behaviour — where they apply. Vague language like "being inappropriate" is not acceptable where a more precise term applies.
5. Apply consequences consistently  The same behaviour must attract the same response, whoever the child is.
6. Protect the victim  A child who reacts to sustained harassment must not be sanctioned without full consideration of what they have been experiencing. Support — welfare check-ins, counselling, adjusted arrangements — must be offered.

Your rights as a parent

To be formally notified in writing when a safeguarding protocol is opened in relation to your child
To know who the designated safeguarding coordinator is and how to contact them
To receive written responses to formal concerns within the stated timeframe
To access all data held about your child under GDPR (Subject Access Request)
To escalate to the Conselleria d'Educació or Education Inspectorate if the school's response is inadequate

Annual accountability
Every year the school will provide parents with an anonymised report covering the number of formal protocols opened, the categories of behaviour recorded, the outcomes, and any changes made to the policy. The review will consider pupil, parent and staff feedback.
